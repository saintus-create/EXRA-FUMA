If, like me, you received your ADHD diagnosis in adulthood, it likely brought with it both answers and questions. Many of the questions may relate to your career, and whether you are in the right one.

Research has found that prior to diagnosis, individuals perceive their career decisions to have been made impulsively. They also reported that they have historically engaged in minimal career planning activities, and this has resulted in 'poor' outcomes.

Their diagnosis became a turning point and provided them with the opportunity to reflect upon their career, and in some cases, construct a new career based on this diagnosis (Kugler, 2025).

## Four stages

This same study identified four stages through which individuals pass after receiving an ADHD diagnosis in adulthood relating to career:

1. **grief**
2. **acceptance**
3. **intention**
4. **action**

Whilst many engage in stages 1 and 2, fewer engage in stages 3 and 4 (Kugler, 2025). In other words, you know something needs to change, but what and how?

## What needs to change?

As children and adolescents, our parents / caregivers are one the most influential forces upon our career choice (McMahon & Patton, 2022).

As adults however, we are often left to make these big career decisions ourselves, despite never being taught how to do so.

Add to this more responsibilities, a brain that is excellent at big picture, abstract thinking but may not be as adept at systemic decision-making (Mowinckel et al., 2015), and the greatly increased likelihood of burnout in the ADHD community (Turjeman-Levi et al., 2024), and it is not hard to see why many do not progress to the intention and action stages.

## Will a change affect my prospects?

Another concern is 'will my resume look messy with (another) career change?'

This is a valid concern however, research shows that the average person will have 5 different careers across 17 different employers in their lifetime (Austin et al., 2022).

That is true for both neurotypical and neurodivergent people.

## How to decide

When making big decisions, I am a huge fan of a **decision-making framework**. This protects me from 'shiny new thing' syndrome (the propensity to follow a certain path because it seems interesting, without considering all the factors).

When it comes to career, the most important thing is that it fits in with our life as it is and as we want it to be. How do we do this? By identifying our values and our goals (Amundson et al., 2020).

## A framework for career decisions

1. **Identify your values** – What matters to you? What do you stand for? What won't you compromise on?

2. **Set your goals** – Short, medium and long term. What do you want to achieve in your career? What does success look like for you?

3. **Consider your interests** – What do you enjoy? What are you naturally drawn to? What could you spend hours learning about?

4. **Identify your accommodation needs** – What do you need to work at your best? Flexible hours? Remote work? Quiet environment?

5. **Find your 'right difficult'** – Our brains need challenge and novelty, so find work that both interests and challenges you. (Hallowell, 2023)

6. **Person-Environment Fit** - in ADHD terms, this relates to a career that can work with your accommodation needs. For example, if you need a hybrid working environment, a career in frontline emergency services may not meet this requirement. (Holland, 1997)

7. **The work is the reward** – Given our propensity for social justice thinking, a career that aligns to your values will help you stay engaged even during more mundane tasks – seeing the why in our work is the key. (Hallowell, 2023)

Once you have your values, goals, interests, accommodations, and your why listed, you can firstly assess your current career. Maybe with a few tweaks you can have the work life you really want!

Alternatively, maybe a change is on the cards. If so, use the above to guide your decisions and avoid the 'shiny new thing syndrome' that has almost led me into many weird and wonderful careers!
