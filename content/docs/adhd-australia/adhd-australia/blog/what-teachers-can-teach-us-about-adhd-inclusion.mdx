> *By Flynn Vamos and Melanie Porter*

As both psychology researchers and parents of children with ADHD, when we set out to research how inclusion plays out for students with ADHD in Australian primary schools, it was clear we needed to ask teachers.

Our study was the first in Australia to interview primary school teachers specifically about ADHD and inclusion, which matters because primary school is where attentional differences are often first noticed[^1] and when ADHD is most commonly diagnosed[^2].

What teachers shared was clear, consistent, and practical.

## ADHD - the blindspot of inclusion

First, they described ADHD as the 'blind spot' of inclusion. In classrooms, behaviours linked to ADHD were often misread as defiance or laziness, particularly when teachers' knowledge of ADHD was limited. That misrecognition could trigger a cycle.

If behaviour is judged as controllable, responses trend toward punishment instead of support. Diagnostic overshadowing made this worse - when ADHD co-occurred with other conditions like autism or anxiety, ADHD needs were frequently sidelined.

Teachers also described how inattentive presentations, especially in girls, were easily missed, only to face mounting gaps in learning and self-esteem later.

## Invisibility in practice

Second, teachers spoke about invisibility in practice. ADHD can look different in any given moment and from child to child, which makes needs less obvious than those associated with more visible challenges and diagnoses.

Several teachers worried that when supports are scarce and within the backdrop of increasingly diverse classrooms, that their attention may be pulled towards the students whose needs are most visible or urgent.

They feared that children with less visible needs such as those associated with ADHD, could drift to second or third priority, despite the significant impact on learning, belonging, and wellbeing.

## Practical barriers

Third, teachers highlighted practical barriers and discussed what actually helps. Large classes, staffing pressures, and limited time made it difficult to give a dysregulated child the one-to-one support that can turn a day around.

Traditional professional development often felt superficial, if offered at all. What teachers asked for was specific and doable: short, targeted training on ADHD; in-class modelling and coaching; and easier access to allied-health input that fits real classroom routines.

They also stressed the importance of working with students and letting them have a say in their learning, co-setting goals, and finding paths that play to their individual strengths.

## Relationships are key

Amid these challenges, the heart of effective inclusion was unmistakable. Relationships.

Teachers described how noticing a child early, greeting them warmly, learning their triggers, and building trust with families can transform engagement.

Many drew on lived experience as adults or as parents of children with ADHD, and how this helps them see beyond the surface and respond with patience, empathy, and care.

## What can schools do now?

So, what can schools do now? From teachers' insights, three practical moves stand out:

1. Build diagnostic literacy and targeted strategies through short, accessible training focused on the different ways ADHD can manifest
2. Bring support to where learning happens with in-class modelling or coaching, and streamlined input from psychologists, occupational and speech therapists
3. Prioritise relationships and student voice - know the child early, co-design adjustments, and celebrate strengths

Inclusion is more than a word. It's the sum of many small, informed actions. When schools make those actions easier for teachers, students with ADHD don't just 'get by', they can truly thrive.

## About the authors

### Flynn Vamos

Flynn is a mother of three primary school children diagnosed with ADHD. Currently completing her honours in psychology at Macquarie University. Working alongside Associate Professor Melanie Porter, their research explored how Australian teachers navigate the inclusion of ADHD students in mainstream classrooms against the backdrop of inclusive educational reform.

### Melanie Porter

Melanie is a senior clinical neuropsychologist, associate professor and director of ADHDA. She supervised Flynn's honours research. Melanie is also a parent of a teenage boy with ADHD and has seen the struggles of ADHD students, parents and teachers first hand.
