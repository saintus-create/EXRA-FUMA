Standing on the sidelines of a basketball court recently, I struck up a conversation with another parent. She was struggling with the challenge of two very different sons. Her eldest is thriving; he's a natural athlete and social butterfly. Her second son, however, is very different and is struggling to find his place in the world. He doesn't enjoy team sports, struggles to connect, and feels the weight of his brother's shadow. She was searching for a place where he could just be himself.

**Without hesitation, I suggested Scouts.**

All three of my boys have been through the scouting program. What I love about it is its inherent inclusivity; you don't need to be the fastest runner, the top of the class, or "mainstream" to participate. The mission of Scouts promotes self-belief, community contribution, and a "give it a go" attitude. While it attracts many children who may not fit in elsewhere, it isn't a "misfits club." It's simply a space where kids can be themselves while navigating the world alongside others doing the same.

I recently attended a Mother's Day event at our local Scout hall. It was the first time since my third son joined that I had actively participated rather than just doing the "drop and go," and it was an eye-opening experience.

As a parent, it can be physically painful to watch your child navigate social waters. I watched my son approach a group of kids playing a game of tip. He went up and tipped someone, but the other child just looked confused. He tried running around the group, waiting to be chased, but no one followed. I could see him trying to figure out the "code" of the game, but he couldn't quite crack it.

I felt the urge to pull him aside and coach him: "Ask if you can play first," or "Don't touch people you don't know." But then I stopped. I've always struggled with group dynamics myself. Watching him took me back to my own childhood, the loneliness of being picked last, the confusion of unwritten rules that everyone else seemed to understand instinctively.

Instead of intervening, I watched. And what I saw was beautiful.

One of the older Scouts noticed him struggling. Instead of ignoring him or correcting him, this teenager simply included him. He changed the game slightly, made the rules clearer, and suddenly my son was part of the group. No fuss, no drama, just quiet inclusion.

This is what Scouts does best. It creates a community where neurodivergent kids can:

- **Try new things without fear of failure** - The "give it a go" attitude means mistakes are part of learning
- **Build genuine connections** - Through shared activities and challenges
- **Develop practical skills** - From camping to cooking to first aid
- **Find their strengths** - Every child has something to contribute
- **Be themselves** - No pressure to fit into a narrow definition of success

For parents of ADHD children, finding spaces where our kids can just be kids - where their differences are celebrated rather than pathologised - is invaluable. Scouts provides exactly that.

It's not perfect, and every troop is different. But when you find the right fit, it can be transformative. My son didn't just learn to play tip that day - he learned that he belongs. And that's worth more than any badge.

---

If you're considering Scouts for your ADHD child, here are some tips:

1. **Talk to the leaders** - Let them know about your child's needs and strengths
2. **Visit first** - See if the troop culture feels right for your family
3. **Start small** - Consider a trial period before committing
4. **Stay involved** - Volunteer if you can - it helps you understand the environment
5. **Be patient** - It may take time for your child to find their place

Scouts isn't for everyone, but for many neurodivergent children, it offers something rare: a place where they can discover their strengths, build genuine connections, and simply be themselves.
